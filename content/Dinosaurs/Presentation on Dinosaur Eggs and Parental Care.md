- Reproduction of dinosaurs? 
- Hadrosaurs, ceratopsians and pachycephalosaurs, ceratosaurs possessed elaborate head gear - horns, crests, shields and spikes - theorized that they used these as defense - but now believed that they used these for visual display - mating.
- Cloaca - There are no fossil records of any dinosaur reproductive organs, let alone a fossilized version of two ancient creatures caught in the act of copulation. Both male and female reproductive parts are formed of soft tissues which perish over time. Most of what we know - comes from extant relatives - cloacal kiss - reqd dinosaurs to arrange themselves in weird positions to make it work

- Eggs of dinosaurs
- What features did they have? - Archosaurian eggshell  - thick enough to provide the calcium needed by the developing skeleton - strong enough to withstand mechanical stress. It must also be thin enough to allow the conductance of respiratory gases and water vapor without inhibiting hatching
- Pores in the eggshell provide for the diffusion of gases, and the inner surface of the shell is apparently resorbed for skeletal calcium
 - Pores in eggs allow the conductance of water vapor and respiratory gases - Eggs in subterranean nests have more pores -  The number and geometric patterns of pores have been used to estimate environmental conditions during incubation of extinct taxa
 - Surface Patterns Many dinosaur eggs have a variety of surface patterns
 - highly textured crenellations and deeply undercut caverns (Jensen 1970). The purpose of these structures is not well understood (Mikhailov 1997a), and the structures have no modern analogs
 - Why were these present? - suggested that the surface ridges also may have  been useful for increasing overall shell thickness and strength without extending pore length
 - Shape of the eggs? - Egg shape in reptiles and birds is thought to be regulated by resistance within the oviducts (Iverson & Ewert 1991, Smart 1991). Extant non-avian reptiles ovulate and shell several eggs at a time, whereas birds ovulate and shell a single egg - Nearly all non-avian dinosaur eggs are symmetrical except Troodon and Oviraptor eggs, which are elongated and asymmetrical
 - Colour of the eggs! - Birds are the only living amniotes with coloured egg, we thought they were the origin of this trait - but the pigments responsible for all the variation in avian egg colour—in fossilized eggshell of a nonavian dinosaur (2017 paper)
 - Because their eggs were exposed, eumaniraptorans may have had good reason to color them with hues and speckles -  Unlike humans, which have three types of light-sensitive cells in their eyes, birds and dinosaurs had four and so would have perceived color differently from us. - the dinosaur _Deinonychus_ probably had blue eggs - high-resolution Raman microspectroscopy to eggshells -  egg colour pigments were preserved in all eumaniraptorans: egg colour had a single evolutionary origin in nonavian theropod dinosaurs. The absence of colour in ornithischian and sauropod eggs represents a true signal rather than a taphonomic artefact.

- Laying eggs
- An oviraptosaurian specimen from an Upper Cretaceous formation in China retains a pair of shelled eggs in the pelvis, providing direct evidence that oviraptorosaurian dinosaurs laid paired elongatoolithid eggs - had two functional oviducts (like crocodiles) but that each oviduct produced only one egg at a time and that an entire egg clutch was laid through multiple ovipositions (like birds). The orientations of the eggs inside the skeleton and in clutches indicate that the mother came to the center of the nest to lay eggs. (2005)
- the estimated 74 days of incubation for _Troodon_ appears to be clearly intermediate between the incubation periods of extant birds and reptiles. **Incubation** (2018)
- Our results suggest that the smallest oviraptorosaurs probably sat directly on the eggs, whereas with increasing body size more weight was likely carried by the central opening, reducing or eliminating the load on the eggs and still potentially allowing for some contact during incubation in giant species. This adaptation, not seen in birds, appears to remove the body size constraints of incubation behaviour in giant oviraptorosaurs. - Smaller species sat on the eggs, larger ones sat on the central gap inside the clutches. 

- Nests
- Eggs that appear scattered and random or in large arcs may have been made by quadrupedal animals that invested minimal energy, whereas well-organized clutches may have been laid by  
bipeds and manipulated with their ‘‘hands’’
- troodontid rims - This rim marks the edge of a nest structure that was constructed independent of the egg-laying process, and it documents an added  parental investment in delineating a particular area around the clutch. The rim likely reduced flooding and predation/Hadrosaurs also used mudballs for the rims

- Parental Care in dinosaurs - 
- Maiasaura - good mother lizard - evidence for feeding young and caring for them - still debated
- Titanosaurs and bigger sauropods were far too big compared to their young - and would have probably crushed them. It has been inferred from a 2023 paper that titanosaurs showed an absence of parental care similar to turtles - they laid a plethora of eggs and abandoned them - letting them fend for themselves. 
- MISNOMER OF Oviraptor - The first oviraptor was discovered in Mongolia in 1922. It was given its name, which means “egg thief”, because it was found near a nest of what appeared to be _Protoceratops_ eggs -  fossilised embryo in an identical egg - recognised it as a kind of oviraptor, suggesting the original thief was in fact a parent.
- ‘The dinosaur was caught in the act, curled up on its nest. It could have been caught up in a sandstorm or a mudslide and was buried with its eggs - that is definitely protective behaviour to the detriment of the parent.’  - oviraptor put itself on the line for protecting its young.
- The most direct evidence of parent/offspring relationships are the occurrences of  
	adult Oviraptor and Troodon sitting on their nests - Two localities have provided data from which parental feeding of young has  
	been hypothesized. One is based on the presence of nestlings incapable of strenuous locomotion

- Sexual dimorphism, sexual display, nest construction, aggregate nesting, egg  
	guarding, and parental care (protection) of the young are all first order inferences  
	for extinct dinosaurs because these behaviors are observed in most extant archosaurs 
- Parental feeding of altricial (semi-altricial) young is a second order inference, exhibited by various birds but not by crocodilians.
- If Maiasaura fed its young, it is possible that this behavior evolved twice—once among some hadrosaurs or a broader group within ornithischians, and again among various birds (and perhaps a wider group within saurischians).
