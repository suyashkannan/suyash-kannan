## Painting the picture
Insects were feeding on the rotting diplodocus carcass that lay near the shore, the poor juvenile sauropod had been abandoned by its herd, because its deformed limb was slowing the herd down. It fell prey to a pack of cunning ceratosaurs when it went in for a sip of water. The pack had had its fill and were taking turns guarding the carcass from scavengers. A small stokesosaurus (a relative of *Tyrannosaurus rex*) was eyeing the carcass from behind the bushes. It trotted towards the carcass, laying low in order to avoid being seen, when it heard the padded footsteps of a large predator behind it, before it could even turn to face the source, a set of large dagger-like teeth dug deep into its torso, crushing its rib cage and swallowing the small theropod whole. A giant theropod, bigger and scarier than anything the Morrison creek had seen before, roared, its roar alerted all the nearby creatures and the ceratosaurs bolted at its sight. No therapod in its right mind would dare to take on the King of the Jurassic, *Saurophaganax maximus*. The stokesosaurus was but a simple snack, the diplodocus carcass was of more interest to the Saurophaganax. It was more than capable of hunting a juvenile sauropod such as this, but a free meal was always welcome.

## Introducing the Chimera
The largest predator of the Jurassic, *Saurophaganax maximus* ("Lord of lizard-eaters") was a deadly ambush hunter, an apex predator of its habitat. It was around 10-13 meters tall, nearly the size of a T. Rex (which came after 50 million years later, in the Cretaceous period), and almost half the mass, around 3000-4500 kg. It possessed a rugged crest above its eyes, which perhaps was used to identify members of the same species. It bore close resemblance to Allosaurus, but was different enough to be a species of its own. Unfortunately, recent studies have given enough data to discard Saurophaganax as a species. 

![[Pasted image 20250307230240.png]]

![[Pasted image 20250307230310.png]]

Discovered in the 1930s in the Kenton quarry of the Morrison creek formation in Oklahoma by paleontologist John Willis Stovall, the therapod was initially named *Saurophagus maximus* ("Lizard Eater"). It was realized 60 or so years later, that this name had already been in use for a living species of birds - The Great Kiscadee. Hence, the name *Saurophaganax maximus* was introduced. It was a large Allosaurid, making up less than 1% of the fossils found in the Morrison creek, suggesting that this predator was either very rare, or was a visiting this area when it got fossilized. 

There was a lot of confusion in the name given to this large carnivore. A paper describing this therapod written by Daniel Chure called it *Saurophaganax maximus*, while David Smith's paper called it *Allosaurus maximus*. Perhaps the similarity of these two species resulted in such a mishap. But one thing was for sure, Saurophaganax was **not** an Allosaur.
## Analyzing the Holotype
The holotype that described *Saurophaganax maximus* consisted of a Dorsal Neural Arch, and Atlas (neck bone) and some tail vertebra called Chevrons. The Atlas was a Saurischian feature, meaning both Sauropods and Therapods possessed similar looking atlas bones. Comparing this bone with that of an Allosaur, a very clear difference could be seen. It was distinct enough to be considered a separate species. Upon comparison with a Camarasaurus bone, an unnatural similarity could be seen. Which led to the conclusion that this bone might have belonged to a Camarasaurus. Thus making Saurophaganax a [[Chimera]]. The meat cleaver-esque chevrons were not present in Allosaurs, and were believed to be convergent evolution to tyrannosaurids. However, such chevrons also belonged to Diplodocus, a sauropod which had a very high population in the Morrison creek formation. The dorsal neural arch was a characteristic of Apatosaurs and Carcharadontosaurids, thus increasing the ambiguity. Sadly, *Saurophaganax maximus* has now become a [[Nomen dubium]], as there is a lot of ambiguity in the holotype. If a more complete fossil that contained those meat cleaver-esque chevrons was discovered, Saurophaganax could return to being a species of its own.

## Another King
A new Allosaurid was in fact, present in the Kenton quarry. It was different from the other two Allosaurs found in the Morrison creek, and was named *Allosaurus anax* ("Allosaur King") as a way of paying homage to *Saurophaganax maximus*.

![[Pasted image 20250307230659.png]]
## Conclusion
In a nutshell, the chimeric holotype that once described Saurophaganax, consisted of mostly sauropod bones, which upon analysis resulted in the declaration of this species as a nomen dubium. If more data can be gathered, it could return to being a species of its own, but that is something that only time can tell. Its legacy still lives on, but with a different name, *Allosaurus anax*, although not as big or as heavy, it was still a formidable predator and just as beautiful as *Saurophaganax maximus*.
