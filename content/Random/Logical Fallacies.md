## Formal Logic:
Let us look at the following argument:
	If x is U --> then x is G
	and a is U
	therefore a is G.

In the **classical approach** - The argument -(-A) would imply A is true.
	True or False - 2 possibilities
In the **three valued logic** - This wouldn't be true.
	 True, False and Something in between

1.  *Denying the Antecedent*:
		- If A implies B
		- and A is false
		- Then B is false.
	- This line of reasoning is invalid. 
	- Most of the fallacies discussed are of this form.
2. *Fallacy of Composition*:
		- inferring something about the objects or collections of objects from properties about the objects themselves.
		- this fallacy can sometimes lead you to the right conclusion, but it isnt guaranteed to do so.
		- Every X in Y holds property P
		- So the collection Y holds property P.

Validity and Soundness of an Argument:
Sound argument: premises of the argument are true
If an argument is valid --> are the assumptions of the argument true?
if they are, then for sure the argument is true.

3. *ad hominem*:
		- attacking a person's character instead of their ideas.

## Informal Logic:
 1. *No true Scotsman fallacy*:
		- shifting your definition in order to defend a point
		- this breaks the [[Rules of Argumentation]] as you are shifting your position without conceding to your opponent. 
2. *Motte and Bailey fallacy*:
		- you have two arguments disguised as one
		- motte refers to your 'backup' argument which pulled out when your bailey argument is countered/questioned.
		- Generally bailey refers to a more extreme argument, while motte is a more sensible and modest position.
		- again we are breaking the [[Rules of Argumentation]] by not being clear about our position.
3. *Burden of proof fallacy*:
		-  forcing the opponent to take the burden of proof when we are the ones obligated to do so.
		- generally if an argument wishes to persuade people, it required disproving rather than the burden beginning on the more 'accepted' argument to prove itself.
4. *Strawman fallacy*:
		- dishonesty representing your opponents standpoint, breaks the rules of arg.
		- extrapolating what the opponent says to make his standpoint sound absolutely absurd.

## Beyond Logic and Fallacies
1. *The 'Fallacy' fallacy*:
		- simply naming the fallacy used by the opponent does not dismiss their argument, and cannot be used to deem their conclusion as false.
2. Rather than following this named fallacy system, as these fallacious arguments could make a justifiable point, we should ask ourselves a bunch of questions in order to be able to dismiss an opponent's claim as fallacious.
		1. Is this argument valid?
		2. are there any obviously unsound premises?
		3. are they acting as if they want an honest discussion of ideas? or have they fallen afoul of an argumentative rule?
		4. With what strength is the conclusion being argued for? Plausibility, probability, proof or something else?
		5. What level of strength do the premises actually give to the conclusion? Does is match the arguer's intention or have they overplayed their hand?
		6. What have your opponent's premises shown, if they are true, regardless of their intention? Do you need to adjust your argument, or your beliefs on this basis?
		7. Under what circumstances would your opponents argument be correct?
