## **1. Introduction to Cells**

- **Cell Theory**:
    
    - All living organisms are composed of cells.
        
    - Cells arise from pre-existing cells (Rudolf Virchow, 1855).
        
- **Diversity of Cells**:
    
    - Prokaryotes (e.g., _E. coli_, _Helicobacter pylori_).
        
    - Eukaryotes (e.g., _Saccharomyces cerevisiae_, mammalian neurons).
        
    - Size range: ~1 µm (bacteria) to ~100 µm (human cells).
        

---

## **2. Light Microscopy**

### **A. Brightfield Microscopy**

- **Principle**: Light passes through a specimen; contrast is enhanced via staining.
    
- **Components**:
    
    - Light source → Condenser lens → Objective lens → Ocular lens.
        
- **Applications**:
    
    - Viewing stained tissues (e.g., H&E staining for histology).
        
- **Limitations**:
    
    - Low contrast (requires staining).
        
    - Cannot observe live cells clearly.
        

### **B. Phase Contrast & DIC Microscopy**

- **Phase Contrast**:
    
    - Converts refractive index differences into brightness variations.
        
    - Used for live, unstained cells (e.g., observing organelles).
        
- **Differential Interference Contrast (DIC)**:
    
    - Provides pseudo-3D images with edge enhancement.
        
    - Better resolution than phase contrast.
        

### **C. Fluorescence Microscopy**

- **Principle**:
    
    - Fluorophores absorb light at one wavelength and emit at another.
        
- **Key Components**:
    
    - Excitation filter, dichroic mirror, emission filter.
        
- **Applications**:
    
    - Protein localization (immunofluorescence).
        
    - Tracking molecular interactions (FRAP, FRET).
        
- **Advanced Techniques**:
    
    - **Confocal Microscopy**: Optical sectioning for 3D imaging.
        
    - **Super-Resolution Microscopy** (STED, STORM, SIM): Breaks diffraction limit (~20–100 nm resolution).
        

---

## **3. Electron Microscopy**

### **A. Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM)**

- **Principle**: Electrons pass through ultra-thin sections (~50–100 nm).
    
- **Sample Prep**:
    
    - Fixation → Dehydration → Embedding → Sectioning → Heavy metal staining (e.g., uranyl acetate).
        
- **Resolution**: ~1–2 nm.
    
- **Applications**:
    
    - Ultrastructure of organelles, viruses, and macromolecules.
        

### **B. Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)**

- **Principle**: Electrons scan the specimen surface; scattered electrons form a 3D image.
    
- **Resolution**: ~10 nm.
    
- **Applications**:
    
    - Surface topography of cells, tissues, and materials.
        

### **C. Cryo-Electron Microscopy (Cryo-EM)**

- **Principle**: Samples frozen in vitreous ice; imaged in near-native state.
    
- **Advantages**:
    
    - No staining/fixation artifacts.
        
    - Atomic-level resolution (e.g., ribosomes, viral capsids).
        
- **Techniques**:
    
    - **Single-Particle Analysis**: Averages thousands of particle images.
        
    - **Tomography**: Tilts specimen for 3D reconstruction.
        

---

## **4. Immunoelectron Microscopy**

- **Principle**: Antibodies labeled with electron-dense gold particles bind to target proteins.
    
- **Applications**:
    
    - Subcellular protein localization at high resolution.

## **5. Comparison of Microscopy Techniques**

|**Microscope Type**|**Resolution**|**Magnification**|**Sample Prep**|**Live Imaging?**|**Key Applications**|
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
|**Brightfield**|~200 nm|Up to 1000x|Staining|No|Histology, fixed cells|
|**Phase Contrast**|~200 nm|Up to 1000x|Unstained|Yes|Live cell imaging|
|**Fluorescence**|~200 nm|Up to 1000x|Fluorophores|Yes (if live dyes)|Protein tracking|
|**Confocal**|~200 nm|Up to 1000x|Fluorophores|Yes|3D cell imaging|
|**Super-Resolution**|20–100 nm|Up to 100,000x|Fluorophores|Yes (some)|Nanoscale protein mapping|
|**TEM**|1–2 nm|Up to 1,000,000x|Heavy staining|No|Organelle ultrastructure|
|**SEM**|~10 nm|Up to 500,000x|Metal coating|No|Surface morphology|
|**Cryo-EM**|~3 Å (0.3 nm)|Up to 1,000,000x|Frozen, unstained|No (but near-native)|Atomic structures|

---

### **Key Takeaways**

- **Light Microscopy**: Best for live-cell imaging, limited by diffraction (~200 nm).
    
- **Electron Microscopy**: Ultra-high resolution but requires complex sample prep.
    
- **Super-Resolution & Cryo-EM**: Bridge the gap between light and electron microscopy for molecular-scale imaging.
