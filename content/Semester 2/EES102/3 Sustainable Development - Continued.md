#### **1. Ecological Footprint**

- **Definition**: Measures the demand humans place on nature, expressed in **global hectares (gha)**.
- **Components**:
  - Cropland.
  - Grazing land.
  - Fishing grounds.
  - Forests.
  - Built-up land.
- **Implication**: Humanity’s footprint exceeds Earth’s biocapacity, requiring **1.7 Earths** to sustain current consumption.

---

#### **2. Throughput**

- **Definition**: The amount of material or resources that flow through a system.
- **Example**:
  - A high-consumption household brings in and disposes of large amounts of resources.
  - A low-consumption household produces minimal waste.
- **Significance**: Describes resource use and waste generation in systems.

---

#### **3. Ecosystem Services**

- **Definition**: Resources and services provided by environmental systems, often invisible and unpriced.
- **Types**:
  1. **Provisioning Services**: Fuel, food, water.
  2. **Supporting Services**: Water purification, oxygen production.
  3. **Regulating Services**: Climate regulation, carbon capture.
  4. **Cultural Services**: Recreation, aesthetic value.
- **Importance**: Essential for human survival and well-being.

---

#### **4. Tragedy of the Commons (Garret Hardin, 1968)**

- **Concept**: Overuse and destruction of shared resources (e.g., pastures, fisheries, clean air) due to population growth and lack of regulation.
- **Solutions Proposed by Hardin**:
  1. **Private Property**: Owners protect resources out of self-interest.
  2. **Coercive Regulation**: Government intervention to manage resources.
- **Critique**: Hardin’s framework assumes individuals act selfishly and ignores community-based solutions.

---

#### **5. Managing the Commons (Elinor Ostrom, 2009 Nobel Prize)**

- **Alternative Perspective**: Communities can collectively manage shared resources.
- **Conditions for Success**:
  1. **Effective Monitoring**: Low-cost and efficient oversight of resource use.
  2. **Exclusion of Outsiders**: Prevent overuse by those unfamiliar with local rules.
  3. **Strong Social Networks**: Foster trust and communication among users.
- **Examples**: Community-managed forests, grazing lands, and fisheries.

---

#### **6. Global Commons Management**

- **Hardin’s View**: Local solutions are insufficient; global policy changes are needed.
- **Ostrom’s View**: Invest in local and individual policy changes alongside global efforts.
- **Example**: Climate change requires both international agreements (e.g., Paris Agreement) and local initiatives (e.g., community renewable energy projects).

---

### **Planetary Boundaries**

---

#### **1. Definition**

- **Concept**: Thresholds of environmental change beyond which ecosystems may face irreversible damage.
- **Identified by**: Johan Rockström and colleagues at the Stockholm Resilience Centre.
- **Nine Key Systems**:
  1. Climate change.
  2. Biodiversity loss.
  3. Land system change.
  4. Freshwater use.
  5. Biogeochemical flows (nitrogen and phosphorus).
  6. Ocean acidification.
  7. Atmospheric aerosols.
  8. Stratospheric ozone loss.
  9. Novel entities (e.g., chemical pollution).

---

#### **2. Current Status**

- **Overshoot**: Humanity has exceeded boundaries for climate change, biodiversity loss, and biogeochemical flows.
- **Implications**: Rapid declines in ecosystem services and interconnected global impacts (e.g., deforestation in Southeast Asia affecting North American climate).
- **Interconnectedness**: Planetary boundaries highlight the coupling of global systems and their effects on human well-being.

### **Indigenous Peoples and Biodiversity**

---

#### **1. Role of Indigenous Peoples**

- **Population**: 500 million indigenous people live in traditional homelands.
- **Contribution**:
  - Guardians of biodiversity and undamaged ecosystems.
  - Possess valuable ecological knowledge.
- **Examples**:
  - **Kuna Indians (Panama)**: “Where there are forests, there are native people, and where there are native people, there are forests.”
  - **Countries Recognizing Indigenous Land Rights**: Fiji, Ecuador, Canada, Australia.

---

### **Environmental Justice**

---

#### **1. Definition**

- **Concept**: Combines civil rights with environmental protection to ensure a safe, healthy environment for all.
- **Key Issues**:
  - **Environmental Racism**: Inequitable distribution of environmental hazards based on race.
  - **Toxic Colonialism**: Targeting poor communities in developing nations for waste disposal or risky technologies.

---

#### **2. Evidence of Environmental Injustice**

- **Toxic Waste Sites**:
  - 3 out of 5 African Americans and Latino/as live near uncontrolled toxic waste sites.
  - Fewer than 10% of whites live in such areas.
- **Lead Poisoning**:
  - 4 million children (many from minority communities) have dangerously high lead levels.
- **Global Waste Trade**:
  - Millions of tons of toxic materials are shipped from richer to poorer countries annually.

---

### **What Can You Do?**

1. **Reduce Ecological Footprint**: Adopt sustainable consumption habits.
2. **Support Indigenous Rights**: Advocate for the protection of indigenous lands and knowledge.
3. **Promote Environmental Justice**: Address inequities in environmental hazards and access to resources.
4. **Advocate for Policy Changes**: Support local and global initiatives to manage commons and protect planetary boundaries.
