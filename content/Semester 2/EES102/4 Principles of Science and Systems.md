#### **1. Snapshot Serengeti**

- **Objective**: Study wildlife populations, species interactions, and ecosystem dynamics in the Serengeti.
    
- **Challenges**:
    
    - Vast ecosystem with difficult data collection.

    - Graduate student research.

### **What is Science?**

---

#### **1. Definition**

- **Science**: A systematic process for producing knowledge through observation, experimentation, and logical reasoning.
    
- **Assumptions**:
    
    - The world is knowable.
        
    - Careful observation and testing can reveal truths about the world.
        

---

#### **2. Key Characteristics of Science**

1. **Skepticism**:
    
    - Scientists question existing explanations and remain open to new evidence.
        
2. **Accuracy and Precision**:
    
    - **Accuracy**: Closeness to the true value.
        
    - **Precision**: Repeatability of results.
        
3. **Reproducibility**:
    
    - Results must be replicable by other researchers.
        
4. **Peer Review**:
    
    - Ensures study design, data collection, and interpretation meet high standards.
        

---

#### **3. Reasoning in Science**

1. **Deductive Reasoning**:
    
    - General to specific.
        
    - Example: Knowing gravity, we predict an apple will fall from a tree.
        
2. **Inductive Reasoning**:
    
    - Specific observations to general rules.
        
    - Example: Observing bird migrations to infer seasonal patterns.
        

---

### **Scientific Method**

---

#### **1. Steps in the Scientific Method**

1. **Observation**: Identify a problem or question (e.g., flashlight doesn’t work).
    
2. **Hypothesis**: Propose a testable explanation (e.g., dead batteries).
    
3. **Prediction**: Predict the outcome if the hypothesis is correct (e.g., replacing batteries will fix the flashlight).
    
4. **Experiment**: Test the hypothesis (e.g., replace batteries).
    
5. **Data Collection**: Gather results (e.g., does the flashlight work?).
    
6. **Interpretation**: Analyze results to confirm or refute the hypothesis.
    

---

#### **2. Scientific Theories**

- **Definition**: Well-supported explanations confirmed through repeated testing and consensus.
    
- **Example**: Theory of evolution, climate change theory.
    

---

### **Probability and Uncertainty**

---

#### **1. Role of Probability**

- **Definition**: Likelihood of an event occurring.
    
- **Example**: 20% chance of catching a cold means 20 out of 100 people are likely to get sick.
    
- **Application**: Helps scientists assess risks and make predictions.
    

---

#### **2. Reducing Uncertainty**

- **Strategies**:
    
    - Use large, random samples.
        
    - Compare results to control groups.
        
    - Calculate confidence intervals.
        

---

### **Statistics in Science**

---

#### **1. Why Use Numbers?**

- **Purpose**: Evaluate and compare data.
    
- **Example**:
    
    - Mean particulate level in air: 30 µg/m³.
        
    - EPA standard: 50 µg/m³.
        
    - Conclusion: Air quality is safe.
        

---

#### **2. Confidence Intervals**

- **Definition**: Range within which a parameter is expected to lie with a certain confidence level.
    
- **Example**: Mean air quality of 36.8 µg/m³ with a 95% confidence interval of 35.0–38.6 µg/m³.
    

---

### **Experimental Design**

---

#### **1. Types of Experiments**

1. **Observational Experiments**:
    
    - Observe natural systems without intervention.
        
    - Example: Studying species abundance in the Serengeti.
        
2. **Manipulative Experiments**:
    
    - Actively change variables to test hypotheses.
        
    - Example: Fumigating islands to study species colonization.
        

---

#### **2. Controlled Studies**

- **Purpose**: Isolate the effect of a single variable.
    
- **Example**:
    
    - **Treatment Group**: Tadpoles exposed to lawn chemicals.
        
    - **Control Group**: Tadpoles in identical conditions without chemicals.
        

---

#### **3. Reducing Bias**

1. **Blind Experiments**:
    
    - Researchers do not know which group is treated.
        
2. **Double-Blind Experiments**:
    
    - Neither researchers nor subjects know the treatment groups.
        

---

#### **4. Variables in Experiments**

- **Independent Variable**: The factor being manipulated (e.g., chemical exposure).
    
- **Dependent Variable**: The outcome being measured (e.g., tadpole deformities).
    

---

### **Scientific Models**

---

#### **1. Purpose of Models**

- **Definition**: Simplified representations of complex systems.
    
- **Types**:
    
    - **Mathematical Models**: Equations (e.g., population growth).
        
    - **Simulation Models**: Climate change scenarios.
        
    - **Physical Models**: Wind tunnel tests for cars and airplanes.
        

---

#### **2. Applications of Models**

- **Climate Change**:
    
    - Test effects of CO₂ levels on temperature.
        
    - Incorporate variables like evaporation and cloud cover.
        
- **Energy Balance Models**:
    
    - Study energy flows in ecosystems.
