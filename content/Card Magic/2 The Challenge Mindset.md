A lot of people, laymen of course, take it to heart when a magician fools them with a trick. This is natural, as the main reason people watch card tricks is to figure out the method behind the trick (not that there is a method, it's all 🪄) and this isn't wrong or to be looked down upon. Our scientific temperament is responsible for this; we know for a fact that magic isn't real (we're totally wrong ofc) and that the trick being displayed is a result of years of practice in legerdemain. 

Magicians have to live with this fact. Spectators are going to actively try to figure out your trick, and the slightest fumble would break the illusion. Is there a way to counter this? I'll share a few counters that I picked up from various books and personal experiences.

1. Avoiding the Challenge Mindset - in Darwin Ortiz's book, Strong Magic, he discusses the negative impact of the challenge mindset. If a magician deliberately attempts to 'fool', rather than 'entertain', and berates the audience for not catching the trick, he is going to frustrate the audience. Magic is a cooperative venture, and not a competitive one. The magician needs to portray a trick as a win-win situation. If the magician succeeds in making the trick work, the audience wins by being 'entertained'. I recommend reading this portion from the book.

2. Working on misdirection skills - Avoiding suspicion on your sudden, erratic movements is tough. Applying good misdirection, and providing a logical reason for the movement is key here. If suspicion is aroused, the illusion is broken. 

3. Never repeat a trick - unless you know another method to do it. Repeating a trick is a foolish choice, as the audience knows what to expect, they'll be on the lookout for sleights. The veil of misdirection falls flat on the ground, leaving your  sleights completely naked. The illusion is shattered. This will account for a humiliating experience (I've had this happen to me multiple times). 

4. Know your spectator - if a certain spectator makes it a habit to suspect every move you make, and ruin the experience for the others present, don't perform for them again. You need to choose your spectators wisely. Always be ready to terminate a routine if the spectators are draining your energy. Don't try to fool them in order to prove them wrong. Keeping the illusion intact is more important. 

5. Finally - If you do end up making mistakes, laugh it off and proceed with another one. Practice makes perfect.
