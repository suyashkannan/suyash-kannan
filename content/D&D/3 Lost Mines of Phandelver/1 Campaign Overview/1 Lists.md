- A **cathedral** is a [church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church_\(building\) "Church (building)") that contains the _[cathedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathedra "Cathedra")_ ([Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language") for 'seat') of a [bishop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bishop "Bishop"),[[3 Locations on the Hex Map]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathedral#cite_note-3) thus serving as the central church of a [diocese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diocese "Diocese"), [conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annual_conferences_within_Methodism "Annual conferences within Methodism"), or [episcopate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Episcopate "Episcopate").[[3 Locations on the Hex Map]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathedral#cite_note-NSE-4)
- The main body of the building, making the longer arm of the cross, is called the [nave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nave "Nave"), and is where worshipers congregate; the term is from the Latin word for ship. The cathedral is symbolically a ship bearing the people of God through the storms of life.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathedral#cite_note-15) The nave is also used for major processions, which gather or enter at the furthest door (liturgically generally called the West Door). The aisles on each side of the nave facilitate the movement of people within the building, without disrupting worshipers in the central space.
- The arms of the cross are called the [transepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transepts "Transepts") and often contain a number of chapels. Farthest from the main entry is the _sanctuary_ where the [Blessed Sacrament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blessed_Sacrament "Blessed Sacrament") is laid on the altar or communion table for the consecration
- Cathedrals generally have an area dedicated to the performance of choral services and with seating specifically for the choir and dignitaries of the church and town. This part of the building is called the Choir or Quire, and is generally located between the sanctuary and the nave. Because music often plays an important part in the performance of the liturgy, cathedrals generally have a [pipe organ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pipe_organ "Pipe organ") to accompany the choir.
- The cathedral generally has a [lectern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lectern "Lectern") from which the scripture is read. This often takes the [form of an eagle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eagle_lectern "Eagle lectern") of brass or carved wood which supports the book on its outstretched wings
- The function of expounding on the scriptures is traditionally performed from the [pulpit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulpit "Pulpit"), which is generally constructed in such a way that the voice of the preacher is projected out to the congregation. The act of adding crenels to a previously unbroken parapet is termed crenellation.

# Gods of the region:
1. St. Halbarn the Forlorn - a saint born from a prophecy - who cured the bountiful forest from the plague of blights. The plagued forest had become far too dangerous to venture into - the tribals, lumberjacks, woodworkers, households and villages that depended upon it were driven away. The forlorn saint, who watched his village turn into a viny infestation of blights was so mortified by it that he hacked at every blight that passed him. Over 8 months of non - stop slashing, he found it - the Forlorn Tree of Gulthias - without sleep, food or drink - he managed to rid the forest of the presence of Gulthias. 
2. Gulthias the Bloodthirsty - a terrible lich who once consumed the yir energy of the entire forest - he sealed himself inside the tree of gulthias and spread a blight infestation all across the forests of Westwater. He wished for immortality and the power of gods - which he got. He spread the seeds of more gulthias trees among his followers, he gained god-like power - the only caveat being the fact he was a tree. Halbarn cut him down, and the blights withered into nothingness.
