1. If you have an idea for a character in mind, skip this step.
	1. Think about what you want to role-play as. It could be an alcoholic dwarf out to avenge the death of his wife, or perhaps a noble paladin who happens to be the local hero. If you don't have any ideas as to what you want to play - pick a favorite character from any form of media - films, comics, etc. - and base your character off of them. Or, you could try rolling on some random character tables and generate a random character. Write this vision down on a google doc and follow the next steps.
		- Eg - *My character is based on Severus Snape from Harry potter.*
2. Once you have an idea in mind, you can proceed with the following steps. These steps don't necessarily have to be followed in order, perform them as you brainstorm, and note them in your doc.
	1. Race - the species your character belongs to - its always a good habit to check with the DM before choosing a very rare/exotic race - they might not fit in the DM's vision of the world.
		- Eg - *Drow Elf* 
	2. Class - this defines who your character is, what they are proficient in, what's their fighting style, etc. Don't be overwhelmed by the myriad options present in the rulebook, just pick one you like the sound of, or go with whatever class that best defines the vision for your character. Ignore any combos/builds or meta ideas for now. Pick whatever you like. 
		- Eg - *Wizard*
    3. The best way to come up with backstory and help the DM while you're at it, is to start small. Create 3 npcs (non player characters) that are connected to your character, they could be living people/creatures or dead. 
		1. The bond - Someone your character is attached to - a lover, child, parent, friend, a teacher - anyone your character considers special. Just a name and relation is enough for now.
			- Eg - *Merla, my first love, who died... in the arms of another man.*
		2. The antagonist - Someone/Something who wronged your character/interfered with their plans. A brief description should suffice here. This is the real meat, where the DM can really tie your character with the story and make it feel personal. The antagonist doesn't necessarily have to be an npc, it could be abstract - inner demons perhaps, or the guilt of not saving a loved one - be creative with this. Optionally, you can add a consequence/lasting effect of this relation - trauma, a severed limb, a disability or anything else.
			- Eg - *I couldn't protect her. Memories of her death still haunt me to this day. The mere sight of blood makes me tremble.*
		3. Lastly, the connection - A useful and handy npc, who could offer help/advice when in a bind, or perhaps give the party some crucial information. It could be the local librarian or one of the Baron's butlers - think of logical reasons as to why your character would know them, and how they are connected to your character. 
			- Eg - *Barbellgate the mad wizard, my teacher and once, the strongest mage in the town - he now lies on his deathbed, senseless and senile.*
	4. Great. If you look back, you have now effortlessly come up with the framework of your backstory! You don't need an elaborate 30 page novella of a backstory. All you need is a brief explanation of what makes your character unique. You have the pieces - the villain, the bonds, the connections - use them to come up with a short backstory in 6-15 bullet points, no fancy flavor, just plot points.
			-Eg -   *A Drow elf who lived in the dungeons of the dark elves
			- ventured out to learn magic
			- fell in love with Merla at the magic academy
			- caught the eye of Barbellgate the wizard, who took him in
			- Merla fell in love with Edran, a human spellcaster
			- discriminated for being a Drow at the academy
			- ran away depressed
			- Merla died of sickness, and he wasn't there for her funeral
			- He now roams the lands in search for forbidden magic that can bring back her back, even if it costs the lives of innocents.*
	5. Come up with a brief physical description of your character -Height, skin colour, hairstyle, clothing and body type should prove sufficient. But if words aren't enough - use www.heroforge.com to really get in there and customize your character till it looks like your vision. Download or take a screenshot of it and copy paste it onto your doc. 
			 - Eg - *Tall dark skinned drow elf, long white hair. Drapes a dark woolen cloak, covering his entire body.*
	6. Don't forget to give your character a cool name and maybe a nickname or two.
			- Eg - *Lunair Hissflame, also called Luna by friends*
3. Assuming you have completed all of the above parts, work with your DM during session 0 and work on the meta aspect of character creation, and finally roll/come up with the mannerisms, ideals, bonds and flaws that really make your character feel alive. 
4. Lastly, give your character some flair - maybe do a distinct voice, a catchphrase, or a quirk that really sets your character apart in roleplay.
