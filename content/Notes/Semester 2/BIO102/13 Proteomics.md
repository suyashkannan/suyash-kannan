## **1. Proteomics**

### **Definition & Importance**

- **Proteome**: The complete set of proteins expressed by a cell/tissue.
    
- **Proteomics**: Study of protein identification, quantification, localization, and interactions.
    
- **Human Proteome Complexity**:
    
    - ~20,000 protein-coding genes → >100,000 proteins (due to alternative splicing & post-translational modifications).
        

### **Key Techniques in Proteomics**

#### **A. Mass Spectrometry (MS)**

- **Principle**: Measures mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) of ions.
    
- **Steps**:
    
    1. **Protein Digestion**: Protease (e.g., trypsin) cleaves proteins into peptides.
        
    2. **Ionization**: Peptides are ionized (e.g., MALDI, ESI).
        
    3. **Mass Analysis**: Ions separated by m/z in a magnetic/electric field.
        
    4. **Database Matching**: Peptide masses compared to theoretical spectra for identification.
        
- **Types of MS**:
    
    - **MALDI-TOF**: High-throughput protein identification; used for intact protein mass.
        
    - **ESI-MS**: Analyzes intact molecules; suitable for peptides/small proteins.
        
    - **LC-MS/MS**: Combines liquid chromatography (separation) with tandem MS (fragmentation for sequencing).
        
- **Applications**:
    
    - Protein identification, quantification (e.g., biomarker discovery).
        
    - Post-translational modification (PTM) detection (e.g., phosphorylation).
        

#### **B. X-ray Crystallography**

- **Principle**: X-rays diffract off protein crystals to determine atomic structure.
    
- **Steps**:
    
    1. **Crystallization**: Purified protein is crystallized.
        
    2. **X-ray Exposure**: Diffraction pattern captured.
        
    3. **Structure Modeling**: Computational reconstruction of 3D structure.
        
- **Applications**:
    
    - High-resolution protein structures (e.g., ribosomes, enzymes).
        

#### **C. Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) Spectroscopy**

- **Principle**: Nuclear spins in a magnetic field absorb/emit radiofrequency signals.
    
- **Advantages**:
    
    - No crystallization needed; studies proteins in solution.
        
    - Analyzes protein dynamics (e.g., folding, binding).
        
- **Limitations**: Limited to small proteins (<100 kDa).

## **2. Experimental Models in Cell Biology**

### **A. Prokaryotic Model: _Escherichia coli_**

- **Features**:
    
    - Simple, fast replication (~20 min doubling time).
        
    - Genome: 4.6 Mbp, ~4000 genes.
        
- **Applications**:
    
    - Gene regulation, DNA replication, recombinant protein production.
        

### **B. Unicellular Eukaryote: _Saccharomyces cerevisiae_ (Yeast)**

- **Features**:
    
    - 16 linear chromosomes; genome: 12 Mbp, ~6000 genes.
        
- **Applications**:
    
    - Cell cycle, protein trafficking, apoptosis.
        

### **C. Multicellular Models**

1. **_Caenorhabditis elegans_ (Nematode)**
    
    - **Features**: Transparent body; 959 somatic cells.
        
    - **Applications**: Development, neurobiology, aging.
        
2. **_Drosophila melanogaster_ (Fruit Fly)**
    
    - **Features**: Short lifecycle; 60% gene homology with humans.
        
    - **Applications**: Developmental biology, neurobiology.
        
3. **_Arabidopsis thaliana_ (Plant Model)**
    
    - **Features**: Small genome (125 Mbp); rapid lifecycle.
        
    - **Applications**: Plant development, stress responses.

### **Key Takeaways**

- **Proteomics Tools**: MS dominates protein analysis; X-ray/NMR provide structural insights.
    
- **Model Organisms**: Range from _E. coli_ (simple) to _Drosophila_ (complex), each offering unique advantages for studying conserved biological processes.
    
- **Interdisciplinary Impact**: Combines biochemistry, genetics, and computational biology to decode cellular mechanisms.
