- **Continental Shelf:** This is the gently sloping underwater edge of the continent, covered with sand and gravel. It’s shallow, close to the shore, and rich in marine life—think of it as the ocean’s front porch.
    
- **Continental Slope:** After the shelf, the ocean floor drops steeply. This is like a cliff leading down from the shelf to the deep sea—a rapid transition zone.
    
- **Continental Rise:** Below the slope, the seafloor flattens out again. Sediments from above collect here, making it a gentle incline before reaching the abyss.
    
- **Abyssal Basin:** This is the vast, flat part of the deep ocean floor. Covered with soft muds, clay, and oozes, it makes up most of the deep ocean—consider it the ocean’s “plain.”
    
- **Mid-Ocean Ridge:** A giant underwater mountain chain caused by tectonic plates pulling apart—picture a long, winding backbone across the ocean floor.
    
- **Seamount:** An underwater mountain that doesn’t reach the surface—almost like a submerged volcanic peak.
    
- **Ocean Trench:** The deepest, narrowest parts of the ocean, formed where plates push together and one sinks—imagine long, underwater canyons.
    
- **Island Arc & Back-Arc Basin:** Curved chains of volcanic islands (island arcs) and the sea floor just behind them (back-arc basin), usually found near trenches.

## Plate Tectonics and Intra-Plate Volcanism

- The movement and interaction of tectonic plates shape oceanic basins primarily through processes like seafloor spreading, subduction, and volcanic activity within plates.
    
- Mid-ocean ridges, trenches, volcanic arcs, and seamounts are formed by plate boundaries and volcanic activity inside plates, continuously reshaping the ocean floor.[](https://study.com/academy/lesson/ocean-basins-definition-formation-features-types.html)
    

## Sediment Burden from Various Sources

- **Continental and Extra-Terrestrial Inputs:** Sediments are supplied mainly from continents via rivers, wind, and glaciers transporting sediment to the ocean. Extra-terrestrial inputs, such as micrometeorites and cosmic dust, contribute minorly but add to deep-sea sediment layers.
    
- **Net In-Situ Production:** Sediments are also produced within the ocean itself, especially from the accumulation of shells and skeletons of microscopic plankton organisms (calcareous and siliceous ooze).
    
- **Gravity Mass Flow:** Turbidity currents, underwater landslides, and ice-rafted debris (IRD) transport sediments downslope into deep ocean basins, forming features such as turbidite deposits.[](https://study.com/academy/lesson/ocean-basins-definition-formation-features-types.html)

## Turbidity Currents

Turbidity currents are fast-moving underwater currents that carry sediment down slopes from the continental shelf into the deep ocean. These currents are driven by gravity acting on sediment-laden water, making the water denser than the surrounding clearer water. As they flow downslope, turbidity currents pick up more sediment, accelerating further; when reaching flatter areas like the abyssal plain, sediments settle out, forming deposits called turbidites. They play a major role in transporting terrestrial sediments into deep marine environments.[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turbidity_current)

## Underwater Landslides

Underwater landslides (also called submarine landslides) are mass movements of sediment down the continental slope triggered by earthquakes, sediment overload, or slope instability. These sudden slumps displace large volumes of sediment and can initiate turbidity currents by creating fast-moving sediment plumes. They can reshape the seafloor dramatically and pose hazards to underwater cables and infrastructure.[](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6919390/)

## Ice-Rafted Debris (IRD)

Ice-rafted debris consists of rock and sediment fragments carried by icebergs and sea ice. As the ice moves and eventually melts, these materials are deposited into the ocean. IRD is significant in polar and glacial regions and provides a record of past glaciations and climate changes in sediment layers. It is a mechanism of sediment transport from land to deep sea specifically linked to ice processes

## Lithogenic Sediments

- Made of rock fragments and minerals that come from land (terrigenous origin).
    
- Transported by rivers (fluvial), wind (eolian), ocean currents (advection), gravity (like underwater landslides), and ice (ice rafted debris, IRD).
    
- Example: Sand, clay, and gravel from eroded mountains and volcanoes.
    

## Authigenic Sediments

- Formed directly in the ocean by chemical processes (precipitation).
    
- Include minerals like iron-manganese (Fe-Mn) crusts and nodules, marine barite, carbonates, phosphates, sulfides, and altered clays.
    
- Think of this as minerals "born" in the ocean itself.
    

## Biogenic Sediments

- Made mostly (>30%) from the remains of living things, including shells and skeletons of marine organisms.
    
- Examples: Marine carbonates, phosphates, silicates, barites, sulfides, and organic tissues.
    
- Think "biological leftovers" accumulating on the sea floor.
    

## Cosmogenic Sediments

- Come from outer space, like meteorites and micrometeorites.
    
- Rare compared to others.
    
- Think "space dust and rocks" falling into the ocean.
    

## Easy Mnemonic

- **L-A-B-C**: Lithogenic (land rocks), Authigenic (ocean minerals), Biogenic (biological), Cosmogenic (cosmic space).

- Meteorites are large space rocks that occasionally fall into the ocean.

## Sediment Focusing and Winnowing Explained

- **Sediment Focusing:** This is a process where sediments, transported laterally by currents on the seafloor, accumulate in a particular area in greater amounts than would be expected based purely on vertical settling. It results in sediment being "focused" or concentrated in a basin or depression, often deep-sea basins, due to processes like underwater currents transporting sediments downslope or across seafloor features. This leads to higher sediment accumulation rates than the actual vertical supply from above.
    
- **Winnowing:** This is the natural removal or sorting of fine sediment particles from coarser sediment by flowing water or currents. Winnowing tends to concentrate coarser sediment fractions by preferentially removing finer grains, resulting in better sorted sediments with higher average grain size. It is akin to the agricultural process of separating wheat from chaff, where lighter materials are removed.

## Key Points:

- Both processes are closely linked and influenced by local oceanographic conditions such as bottom currents, seafloor topography, and sediment supply.
    
- Sediment focusing increases sediment deposition through lateral transport and accumulation.
    
- Winnowing causes removal of finer particles, leading to sediment sorting and changes in grain size distribution.
    
- These processes can complicate interpretations of sedimentation rates based solely on vertical sediment accumulation.
    

## Example from Research:

- Measurements using 230230Th normalization show sediment focusing factors greater than 1 where lateral sediment transport enhances accumulation.
    
- Factors less than 1 indicate winnowing or sediment removal from a site.
    
- These processes vary spatially with bathymetry and current flow patterns.
    

Understanding focusing and winnowing is essential for interpreting sediment records accurately, especially when using proxy-based sedimentation rates.[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winnowing_\(sedimentology\))
