Housekeeping genes

• Set of genes that are constitutively expressed in cells and are essential for basic cellular functions.
• Encode for proteins that are involved in fundamental cellular processes, such as cell cycle regulation,
DNA replication, and metabolism.
• Examples- Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH), beta-Actin, ribosomal proteins.


Regulated genes

• Regulated genes are only expressed under specific conditions, allowing cells to respond to
environmental changes or developmental cues.
• Examples- proto-oncogenes, tumor suppressor genes, apoptotic genes, genes involved in DNA repair.

Chemical denaturation: Using chemical agents to disrupt the hydrogen bonds and destabilize the DNA structure.
Common denaturing agents include:
1. High pH: Alkaline conditions cause hydrogen bonds to dissociate. NaOH is a safe, rapid, and accurate chemical
denaturant.
2. Low salt concentrations: Reduced ionic strength can weaken hydrogen bonding.
3. Formaldehyde and urea: These agents can form hydrogen bonds with the nitrogenous bases, interfering with base
pairing.
4. Dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO): Lowers the DNA melting temperature (Tm), facilitating denaturation.

- When heated solutions of denatured DNA are slowly cooled or denaturants are removed, single strands often meet their
complementary strands and re-form regular double helices.

Hybridization: Process in which two complementary
single-stranded DNA from either the same or different
sources anneal together to form a double stranded DNA.

• Hybridization is the basis for several indispensable
techniques in molecular biology, such as Southern
blotting, in situ hybridization, DNA microarray analysis.

- The melting temperature (Tm) is the specific temperature at
which 50% of the DNA molecules in a sample have
separated into single strands.

![[Pasted image 20250916212418.png]]

factors affecting Tm: 1. GC Content: DNA sequences with a higher percentage of guanine-cytosine (GC) base pairs have higher melting
temperatures.

• The higher the percent of G:C base pairs in the DNA (and hence the lower the content of A:T base pairs), the higher is
the melting point.

• G:C base pairs contribute more to the stability of DNA than do A:T base pairs because of the greater number of
hydrogen bonds for the former. And require more energy to denature.

2. Salt Concentration: Increasing salt concentration (e.g., sodium ions) stabilizes the DNA double helix by neutralizing the
negative charges on the DNA backbone. This stabilization requires a higher temperature to break the strands apart, thus
increasing the Tm.

• The higher the salt concentration of the solution, the greater is the temperature at which the DNA denatures.

Covalently closed circular DNA (cccDNA) is a
specialized form of DNA found in the nuclei of
cells infected with certain DNA viruses,
notably the Hepatitis B virus (HBV).

Ø cccDNA is formed from the relaxed circular
DNA (rcDNA) that enters the cell's nucleus and
is then converted to the covalently closed
circular form.
Ø cccDNA acts as a stable template for viral
replication.

In covalently closed, circular DNA (cccDNA), the absolute number of times the chains can twist about each other cannot
change and is said to be topologically constrained.

Linear DNA molecules of eukaryotic chromosomes are subject to topological constraints because of their extreme length,
entrainment in chromatin, and interaction with other cellular components.

Ø If Lk < Lk0 and △Lk < 0, then the DNA is said to be “negatively

Ø If Lk > Lk0 and △Lk > 0, then the DNA is “positively supercoiled.”

Positive and negative supercoiling

• DNA in most eukaryotic and prokaryotic cells is negatively supercoiled.
• Positive supercoiled DNA is observed in certain thermophiles.

What does superhelical density mean biologically?

Negative supercoils can be thought of as a store of free energy that aids in processes that require strand separation,
such as DNA replication and transcription.
Because Lk= Tw+Wr, negative supercoils can be converted into untwisting of the double helix. Regions of
negatively supercoiled DNA, therefore, have a tendency to unwind partially. Thus, strand separation can be
accomplished more easily in negatively supercoiled DNA than in relaxed DNA.
